Phishing and Elections

Local and national political elections are known to bring out heated emotions. With millions of voters actively following the campaign cycle and dedicated supporters invested in the election outcome, phishers take advantage of this time to send out targeted lures.

Emotional Triggers

Spear phishers frequently use current events like elections to entice targets to click links, open attachments, or divulge personal information. To compel targets to take action, phishers appeal to strong emotions like shock, anger, curiosity, fear, and sense of urgency.

For example, phishers often send fake campaign contribution emails to collect fraudulent donations and to steal their recipients’ payment credentials — “Help us reach our donation deadline by tonight!”. Additionally, targets could receive an email thanking them for their recent contribution even though they never donated to the candidate. The shock value of receiving an email for a candidate they dislike could tempt them to click the confirmation link.

Quick Tips

Don’t get caught up in the campaign frenzy. Follow these tips to minimize the risk of falling for a spear phishing email.

  • Always use caution.
    Examine emails carefully, even if the email or website looks legitimate. Phishers often mimic campaign websites and emails.
  • Beware of fake campaign websites.
    To lure targets, phishers buy domain names that seem legitimate or use domain names similar to official campaign sites (ex: www.example.com and www.exampel.com). Although most candidates purchase a variety of domain names in advance, there has been controversy in recent races from candidates failing to secure a domain name. In some cases, attackers use these sites to collect fraudulent donations. Before contributing to any campaign, make sure it is the candidate’s official site.
  • Keep your emotions in check.
    Whether it’s a surprising headline or a fake contribution thank you email, phishers frequently use emotions to compel recipients to open attachments or click links.
  • Check the link.
    Always check the destination of a link before clicking by hovering over it on a desktop, or holding for several seconds on an Android or iOS device.

If you suspect that you have received a spear phishing email at work, follow our procedures for reporting it immediately.

Learn MorePhishing and Elections

Phishers often impersonate government agencies over phone or email, claiming there is a voter registration issue. These fake voter registration emails claim that the recipient must click a link to resolve the issue. Instead, these links go to malicious sites that install malware or ask for personal information. State and county election offices may contact voters via phone or email to conduct official business or request additional information, so always use caution. Find the contact information for the Department of Elections for your state on their official website if you have any questions or concerns.

Other popular scams include prize giveaways or tickets to a campaign event in exchange for answering a political survey. Even if the questions sound legitimate, attackers are looking for personal information and credit card information. Don’t take the bait.
